• The Three Branches of Government

  • Constitution

    When the Constitution was first made in 1787, the Founding Fathers wanted to make sure that there was a separation of powers so that no one person had too much control. This led to the formation of the Three Branches of Government: the Legislative Branch, the Executive Branch, and the Judicial Branch.

    The three branches of government make sure that no one branch has too much power using the process of checks and balances. This means that each branch has the right to amend the acts taken by other branches in order to maintain the balance of power.

  • Legislative Branch

    The Legislative branch is the branch of government that makes laws. It is made up of two parts: the Senate and the House of Representatives.

    The Senate is made up of a total of 100 senators, with 2 senators from each state.

    The House of Representatives has 435 representatives, with the number of representatives from each state dependent on each state's population.

  • Senate and House of Representatives

    Currently there are 232 Republicans, 199 Democrats, and 4 vacancies in the House of Representatives.

    Currently there are 53 Democrats, 45 Republicans, and 2 Independents in the Senate.

  • Executive Branch

    The Executive branch is the branch of government that carries out the laws. It is composed of the President, Vice President, and Cabinet members.

    The President is the head of state, head of the government as well as the commander-in-chief of the military.

    The Vice President supports the president in his roles and is also the presiding officer in the Senate.

    The Cabinet is made up of people nominated by the president and approved by the Senate. They are advisors and heads of various government departments and agencies.

  • Judicial Branch

    The Judicial branch is made up of the government court system and evaluates laws.

    The highest court in the United States is the Supreme Court and is made up of 9 justices, or judges, that are nominated by the President and approved by the Senate.

    There are also lower Federal courts that were later created after the Constitution was written.
